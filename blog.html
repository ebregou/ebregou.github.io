For the next hour, we did some frantic researching of this national park. It looked too beautiful to pass up but there were a lot of lingering logistical questions. Lucky for us, Emily's friend João (officemate from Barcelona) is the GOAT and was able to give us tips from firsthand experience. We love that guy.

After Lena extensively researched all of her concerns using Reddit posts, blogs, and Google street view, she was convinced as was Emily (though Emily was pretty convinced from the beginning). We decided to wake up at 7:15 AM to catch a bus out of Rio's main station in the central zone (we are staying in the South zone). Unfortunately, Emily was partying all night with the mosquitoes in her room and did not get to sleep until very late. She overslept by an hour or so, which gave Lena time to research the buses, find that they were all booked for the times we wanted, and come up with an alternative solution wherein we would take Ubers there and back (which were very reasonably priced for a 2 hour drive). What a win. Good job Lena!

Emily had been wanting to try real açaí which we thought was a totally reasonable breakfast food. I mean you can put granola on the top, so that must be breakfast. The place we went to was definitely an ice cream store complete with candy to put on top of the açaí. We both went crazy, filling our containers to the brim, but Lena at least refrained from putting chocolate on hers.



We had a very friendly Uber driver who let us eat our oversized açaí bowls in the back of his car while cheerfully driving us the 2-ish hours to Teresópolis and attempting to communicate with us in the most confusing possible mix of Portuguese, Spanish, and English. It would definitely have been easier if he stuck to Portuguese; Lena understands Portuguese quite well. He was very perturbed that we were driving past the viewpoints until we explained that we wanted to hike at which point he called us crazy.

The national park is rainforest-y with views of giant protruding cliffs, one of which is known as Dedo de Deus (God's finger).





Everyone on the trail was very friendly and we said a lot of "bom dias" until we were sternly told that it was "boa tarde" now. We even hit some "boa noites" when we got back to Rio so we covered them all.

A big surprise to us was a multi-kilometer suspended bridge section of the trail. Whenever we thought it would end it kept going. Lena did not vibe with the heights, but powered through alternating between saying "This is fine." and "Wait actually this is really cool."

Lena wants everyone to know that this video does not accurately represent the extreme heights of the bridge, nor how slippery / rickety it was.



The national park was lovely and we enjoyed getting out of the city and vibing in nature. While we weren't alone, we were alone as English-speakers with all the other visitors being Brazilian.

When we finished hiking every day hike in the park, we headed into the city of Teresópolis for a snack before our scheduled Uber return trip. To our surprise, the town was full of bougie people wearing high heels. It is Brazilian winter, meaning that not only did everyone have a little sweater on, so did their dogs. We were scared off from the restaurants because they looked fancy and our shoes were covered in mud. The dogs looked better than us. We ended up at a cafe where Lena attempted to order 4 pão de queijos until Emily quickly corrected it to 6 (she doesn't play around about pão de quiejo). We also got some tea and some pasteles de nata. It was just what we needed.



On our Uber back, we got an amazing sunset over the mountains which we captured very inadequately through the tinted windows.


We both passed out in the back of the Uber, each assuming that the other was on top of watching the driver and ensuring we were on course. As we pulled into a gas station and both woke up, we realized that was not the case at which point Lena locked in on staying awake while Emily faded in and out.

Lena, the hero and MAIN CHARACTER of the day, took on cooking some pasta with pesto and tomatoes. We had to boil the tomatoes to make sure they were safe and the kitchen really left a lot to be desired including a lack of cooking oil, no pot big enough for pasta, and only one working burner. Lena powered through and made a delicious-looking meal. Unfortunately, we had accidentally picked up lactose-free cheese from the store (which it said in English on the package so we have no excuses). It was absolutely nasty and quite bitter so that didn't work out too well.

We are SO excited for Karishma to touch down bright and early tomorrow morning! We have a big adventure planned for her jetlagged ass.

Rio de Janeiro 2026, Days 1-2

Day 1

After many delays involving >8 hours in the airport on both their parts, Lena and Emily finally made it to Rio. Lena's plane ran out of fuel before reaching Miami so she got stranded in Fort Myers overnight. Luckily, she got a hotel voucher and $12 off a meal at any single restaurant in the airport. All the food in the airport was closed for the night so she got 2 bottles of water that were somehow $11. Emily was waylaid by under-staffing in Houston and had to delay her entire itinerary by 24 hours. Luckily, they both ended up touching down around the same time and were able to find each other.

Our Airbnb host was a stern but kind lady who was not thrilled about the change of plans. We went rogue for an hour at a beach-side cafe waiting for her to let us in to our apartment. We didn't know enough Portuguese to realize they didn't serve food at this time. We also ran into some very nice security guards who really really wanted to help us get to our apartment. Alas, we did not know enough Portuguese to explain our situation. Almost no one has been speaking English to us. Either people here don't speak English or don't realize we speak English given our attempts in Portuguese (Lena) or Spanish (Emily). Every time Emily has attempted Spanish she has been understood perfectly but the same cannot be said for her understanding of the responses in Portuguese.

Our Airbnb is an airy apartment full of original artwork by the owner who on top of being stern is a bougie art lady. We got some tapioca snacks right across the street which Lena described as having the texture of a chicken cutlet. It's been a really long time since Lena has had chicken cutlets and let's just say... no not at all. We found them to be alright and we also tried queijo de minas which seems to be the Brazilian version of queso fresco.

Trying to order something vegan from the menu in Portuguese was a journey. Emily was just trying her best to avoid vegan cheese (yuck!) Lena ended up with a burger and Emily got meatballs. The waiter was very enthusiastic to present the MEATBALLS or maybe just excited that he knew the word in English. They were decidedly edible.

Day 2

This morning, Lena left early, heading to the university to conduct an interview with an Uber driver (she's here for a research project designing safer and more equitable navigation technology with communities in Rio). Emily, who had been partying with a chorus of mosquitoes last night, accidentally slept in until 11 AM.

The conventional wisdom is not to use your phone on the street lest someone snatch it out of your hands. This makes navigation slightly more fun and difficult-- we have to actually look at the map and remember the way. Lena, having been in Rio for 6 days in April, is already an expert at this. She's also researching the map, which helps. Emily is still getting her bearings, but managed to navigate to a few places all by herself. Regardless of where you are, we think it's a good idea to keep your phone in your pocket and know where you're going if you don't want to look like a noob.

Emily enjoyed her solo wandering-- it's always easiest to blend in with the locals when you're alone. She felt like a Brazilian-- an illusion that was instantly shattered when she had to talk to people. Damn, Portuguese is a whole different language from Spanish. Who knew?

Emily struggled a little to find a place to work but everyone was very nice to her. We have yet to order pao de queijo (one of our favorite Brazilian snacks) because Lena issued a solemn warning that if you mispronounce pao it sounds like dick with cheese rather than bread with cheese. Emily has been practicing all day but couldn't bring herself to order it at breakfast because it was on the kids' menu :(

After getting (nearly no) work done at a cafe, the dynamic duo was reunited to wander the beach and people watch.


You can feel the beach in Rio even if you're not on the beach. Many walk around barefoot with sand in their toes and everyone seems to have just come from the ocean.


People here are so attractive that we feel our attractiveness has increased by proxy. We'll let the pictures do the talking:

They had one cocktail each which is why this blog post is so silly. They were STRONG though!!!!! The fresh fish was amazing and we love Japanese food. #No Ragrets

The fried shrimp bao were to die for.


We got some ice cream on the way back but we have no idea what flavors we got. This is definitely the most challenged Emily has ever felt with language. Everywhere else she's been has either been Spanish-speaking or a language she hasn't even attempted to speak. Portuguese hits that sweet spot for struggle...
